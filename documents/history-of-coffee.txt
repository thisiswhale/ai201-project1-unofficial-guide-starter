History of Coffee

The history of coffee spans many centuries. Wild coffee plants originated in Ethiopia, while the beverage itself has its roots in Yemen, where it was harvested, roasted and brewed; Sufi Muslims in the 15th century used it to aid concentration during night prayers.

From Yemen, coffee spread to Mecca and the wider Arabian Peninsula, and by the early 16th century it had reached Cairo, Damascus, and Istanbul. Debates over its permissibility arose in Muslim society, but it soon became a central part of urban social life.

Through Mediterranean trade routes, coffee entered Europe in the mid-16th century, first in Italy and later in other regions. Coffee houses were established in Western Europe by the late 17th century, especially in Holland, England, and Germany.

Up to the end of the 17th century, the Yemeni port of Mocha was the world's sole gateway for coffee, but as demand grew, cultivation spread to other parts of the world. One of the earliest cultivations of coffee in the New World was when Gabriel de Clieu brought coffee seedlings to Martinique in 1720, from which it spread to other Caribbean islands such as Saint-Domingue and also to Mexico. By 1788, Saint-Domingue supplied half the world's coffee.

By 1852, Brazil became the world's largest producer of coffee and has held that status ever since. Since 1950, several other major producers emerged, notably Colombia, Ivory Coast, Ethiopia, and Vietnam; the latter overtook Colombia and became the second-largest producer in 1999. Today, coffee is one of the world's most popular beverages, with a significant cultural and economic impact globally.

Etymology
The word coffee entered English from the Ottoman Turkish kahve, borrowed in turn from the Arabic qahwah. Qahwah most likely meant 'dark', referring to the brew or the bean: Semitic languages have the root qhh, "dark color". Medieval Arab lexicographers traditionally held that the etymology of qahwah meant 'wine', given its distinctly dark color, and derived from the verb qahiya, 'to have no appetite'. Etymologies include the Ethiopian province of Kaffa in Abyssinia, part of the plant's native region and origin of discovery, and the Arabic quwwa ("power"). The Turkish kahve also inspired caffe in Italian, cafe in French, and koffie in Dutch. The Ethiopian word for coffee is buna, bun, or Buni, which comes from the word for the color brown in Amharic, Tigrinya and Arabic. The terms coffee pot and coffee break originated in 1705 and 1952 respectively.

Genetics
The original domesticated coffee plant is said to have been from Harar, and the native plant population is thought to be derived from Ethiopia with distinct nearby populations in Sudan and Kenya. Genetic studies have been performed on Coffea arabica varieties, which were found to be of low genetic diversity. A 2020 follow-up study definitively confirmed that the vast majority of Coffea arabica plants originate from early varieties in the coffee farms of Yemen.

Spread of Coffee
The earliest mention of coffee noted by coffee merchant Philippe Sylvestre Dufour is a reference to bunchum in the works of the 10th century Persian physician al-Razi. There is no confirmed evidence, either historical or archaeological, of coffee as a drink being consumed before the 15th century. By the late 15th century, coffee drinking was well established among Sufi communities in Yemen. The Sufi Imam Muhammad Ibn Said al-Dhabhani is known to have imported goods from Ethiopia to Yemen.

One of the most important of the early writers on coffee was Abd al-Qadir al-Jaziri, who, in 1587, compiled a work tracing the history and legal controversies of coffee, tracing the spread of coffee from Arabia Felix (present-day Yemen) northward to Mecca and Medina, and then to Cairo, Damascus, Baghdad, and Constantinople. He reported that Jamal-al-Din al-Dhabhani (d. 1470), mufti of Aden, was the first to adopt the use of coffee (circa 1454). Among coffee's properties was that it drove away fatigue and lethargy, and brought to the body a certain sprightliness and vigour.

Sufis in Yemen used the beverage as an aid to concentration and as a kind of spiritual intoxication when they chanted the name of God, and to keep themselves alert during nighttime devotions. By 1414, the plant was known in Mecca, and, in the early 1500s, was spreading to the Mameluke Sultanate of Egypt and North Africa from the Yemeni port of Mocha. Coffee houses appeared in Cairo, in Syria (especially Aleppo), and then in Constantinople in 1554. Coffee was noted in Aleppo by the German physician botanist Leonhard Rauwolf, the first European to mention it, as chaube, in 1573.

In 1511, it was forbidden for its stimulating effect by conservative imams at a theological court in Mecca. These bans were overturned in 1524 by an order of the Ottoman Sultan Suleiman I, with Grand Mufti Mehmet Ebussuud el-Imadi issuing a fatwa allowing the consumption of coffee. From the Middle East, coffee drinking spread to Italy, then to the rest of Europe, and coffee plants were transported by the Dutch to the East Indies and to the Americas.

Yemen
Yemen is regarded as the origin of the beverage and where Coffea Arabica was cultivated as a crop on a large scale rather than gathered from the wild. By the fifteenth century, coffee cultivation had taken root in Yemen's highland regions such as Haraz and Bani Matar, where it was harvested, roasted and brewed by Sufi circles. Coffee was cultivated on terraced slopes overlooking the Tihamah, while caravan routes carried the beans to the Red Sea ports, particularly Mocha. Until the end of the 17th century, Yemen remained the world's principal producer and exporter of coffee, and the port of Mocha became synonymous with the beverage itself.

Legendary Origins
According to one legend, ancestors of today's Kafficho people in the Kingdom of Kaffa were the first to recognize the energizing effect of the coffee plant. One account involves a 9th-century Ethiopian or Arab goatherder, Kaldi, who noticed the energizing effects when his flock nibbled on the bright red berries of a certain bush. He brought the berries to a monk in a nearby monastery; the monk disapproved and threw them into the fire, from which an enticing aroma billowed. The roasted beans were raked from the embers, ground up, and dissolved in hot water, yielding the world's first cup of coffee. This legend does not appear before 1671, indicating the story is likely apocryphal.

Europe
The first European coffee house apart from those in the Ottoman Empire and in Malta was opened in Venice in 1645. Venetian merchants introduced coffee-drinking to the wealthy in Venice. In 1591 Venetian botanist-physician Prospero Alpini became the first to publish a description of the coffee plant in Europe.

Austria: The first coffeehouse in Austria opened in Vienna in 1683 after the Battle of Vienna. Melange is the typical Viennese coffee, served with hot foamed milk. A distinct Viennese coffee house culture developed in the 19th century.

United Kingdom: The first coffeehouse in England was opened in Oxford in 1650. The first in London opened in St. Michael's Alley in Cornhill. By 1675, there were more than 3,000 coffeehouses throughout England. These early English coffee houses became gathering places for religious and political discussion.

France: Coffee was brought to Paris by M. Thevenot around 1657. In 1669, Soleiman Agha, Ambassador from Sultan Mehmed IV, arrived in Paris and helped establish the custom of drinking coffee among Parisians.

Germany: Coffeehouses were first established in North Sea ports, including Hamburg (1677). Johann Sebastian Bach composed the secular "Coffee Cantata" (BWV 211) in Leipzig.

Italy: Coffee arrived in the second half of the 16th century. Pope Clement VIII, after trying the drink, gave it his blessing. In Turin, in 1933, Italian engineer Luigi Di Ponti invented the first moka pot and sold the patent to Alfonso Bialetti.

Netherlands: Pieter van den Broecke, a Dutch merchant, obtained coffee bushes from Mocha, Yemen, in 1616 and took them to Amsterdam's Botanical gardens. In 1658 the Dutch began coffee cultivation in Ceylon (Sri Lanka) and later southern India, then focused on Javanese plantations.

Americas
Gabriel de Clieu brought coffee seedlings to Martinique in 1720. Those sprouts flourished and enabled the spread of coffee cultivation to Saint-Domingue (Haiti), Mexico and other Caribbean islands. By 1788 Saint-Domingue supplied half the world's coffee. Coffee also found its way to the Isle of Bourbon (Reunion); the Santos coffee of Brazil and the Oaxaca coffee of Mexico are progeny of that Bourbon tree. Circa 1727, Francisco de Melo Palheta obtained coffee seeds that commenced the coffee industry of Brazil. After the Boston Tea Party of 1773, large numbers of Americans switched to drinking coffee. Brazil became the largest producer of coffee in the world by 1852 and has held that status ever since, exporting more coffee than the rest of the world combined from 1850 to 1950. Vietnam overtook Colombia and became the second-largest producer in 1999.

Asia
India: Coffee's introduction to India is credited to a Sufi saint named Baba Budan, who planted beans in the Chandragiri hills of Karnataka. The word for coffee is "kaapi" in several South Indian languages. Coffee production is dominated by Karnataka (53%), Kerala (28%) and Tamil Nadu (11%). Coffee is served in a distinctive drip-style "filter coffee" across Southern India.

Japan: Coffee was introduced by the Dutch in the 17th century but remained a curiosity until 1858. By the early 1930s there were over 30,000 coffeehouses. Japan is now one of the leading per capita coffee consumers in the world.

Indonesia: Coffee was introduced by the Dutch in the late 17th century. The colloquial name "Java" comes from the time when most coffee was grown in Java. Today Indonesia is one of the largest coffee producers in the world.

Vietnam: Arabica was first imported in 1857. Cultivation spread to the Central and Western Highlands, which now produce a majority of Vietnam's coffee. The city of Buon Ma Thuot is known as the "coffee capital of Vietnam". Nearly all the coffee grown there is Robusta.

Production
The Dutch supplied Europe's demand with "Java coffee" by 1719 and soon had plantations in Ceylon, Sumatra and other Sunda islands. The first coffee plantation in Brazil occurred in 1727. Brazil relied heavily on slave labor until the abolition of slavery in 1888. For many decades in the 19th and early 20th centuries, Brazil was the biggest producer of coffee and a virtual monopolist in the trade, but high prices opened opportunities for Colombia, Guatemala, Indonesia and Vietnam. Despite the origins of coffee cultivation in Ethiopia, that country produced only a small amount for export until the twentieth century.
